What is Mental Health Learn

Mental health is characterized by our emotional, psychological, and social well-being. It determines how we handle stressful situations, relate to others, and cope with the demands of life (Edwards et al., 2022). Mental health plays a large role in how we think, feel, and act.

Eating Disorders Defined

Athletes may be more prone to eating disorders than the rest of the population. In a study conducted on the risk of disordered eating in college female athletes, it was determined that 76% of female athletes are susceptible to body dysmorphia and eating disorders (Torres-McGehee, 2021).

How to Help Prevention & Recgonizing Signs

As a coach, you are uniquely positioned to detect psychological and physical changes within athletes. As prevention and early diagnosis are key factors for treatment (Heider et al., 2018), it is crucial to recognize symptoms, know how to communicate with your athletes, and be able to provide resources.

Treatment Options Finding Help

Eating disorders stem from various causes and therefore require a multifaceted approach to treatment. Effective treatment plans will typically involve a multidisciplinary treatment team (MDTT) of professionals, including counselors, psychiatrists, dietitians, and medical staff (Carter, 2019; Conviser et al., 2018).

Mental Health & Collegiate Athletes

Mental health encompasses our psychological, emotional, and social welfare and is essential to overall well-being in the same way that physical health is. The importance of mental health tends to be downplayed in western culture and is considered even more taboo in sporting environments. Considering that mental illness is becoming increasingly prevalent among college-aged individuals (Edwards et al., 2022), it is more necessary than ever to be aware of the risk factors, as well as helpful resources.

Collegiate student-athletes with mental health diagnoses have been growing in number over the past several years. This is potentially a result of the ever-growing college-related stressors surrounding the balance of academic, social, and athletic responsibilities (Edwards et al., 2022). Furthermore, the competitive leap from high school to college level athletics may amplify sport-specific pressures to maintain a certain body weight or physique (Arthur-Cameselle et al., 2018). Unfortunately, this can result in preoccupation with body-image and eventually more severe conditions, such as eating disorders.

Studies have suggested that athletes may be more prone to eating disorders than the rest of the population (Baum, 2013). This high rate of proneness is in large part a result of “contextual body image”, which refers to the contradicting body image demands from popular media versus sport (Carter, 2019; Shen, 2022). Those in sports in which lower body fat is advantageous and where they are judged on physique or compete in revealing uniforms, such as swimmers, are at highest risk. Female athletes specifically have reported feelings of extreme awareness of their bodies and other’s evaluations of them while competing in revealing uniforms like swimsuits (Lauer et al., 2018).

Terms & Conditions To Know

Anorexia Athletica: Presenting in female athletes and involving fear of weight gain when underweight. Patients experience weight loss of at least 5% of body weight resulting from limited food intake and compulsive exercise. Other symptoms may include binge eating and pathogenic weight control methods (i.e., self-induced vomiting or use of laxatives or diuretics) (Thompston & Trattner Shermon, 2011).

Anorexia Nervosa – Restricting Type: Extreme fear of weight gain and preoccupation with caloric intake and body image. Maintains low body weight through drastically restricting food intake.

Anorexia Nervosa – Binge/Purge Type: Extreme fear of weight gain and preoccupation with caloric intake and body image. Binge eating and/or purging behaviors are routinely present. Such behaviors may include self-induced vomiting, abuse of laxatives, diuretics, or enemas, and excessive exercise.

Avoidant Restrictive Food Intake Disorder: Also known as selective eating disorder, ARFID is a condition in which the amount or type of food ingested is limited. Unlike anorexia nervosa, there is no fear or preoccupation with weight.

Binge Eating Disorder: Repeated episodes of eating unusually large amounts of food as a result of feeling out of control. Unlike with bulimia nervosa, these binge episodes are not followed by purging behaviors

Body Dissatisfaction: Discontent with entire body, or specific body parts, typically rooted in the belief that one is “fat” or “too big”. Body dissatisfaction plays a substantial role in the development and maintenance of eating disorders.

Body Dysmorphic Disorder: Severe preoccupation with appearance and body image. Intense focus on perceived flaws or defects in appearance that are extremely minor or can not be seen by others. May result in embarrassment, shame, or anxiety surrounding social situations.

Body Image Disturbance: A mindset categorized by a negative perception and attitude regarding physical appearance (Deogracias-Schleich et al., 2022; Heider et al., 2018). Those with BID typically have a misconception that their bodies are bigger than they actually are.

Bulimia Nervosa: Recurrent and frequent episodes of eating unusually large amounts followed by compensation behaviors like fasting, self-induced vomiting, abuse of laxatives, diuretics, or enemas, and excessive exercise. Those suffering may be slightly underweight, normal weight, or overweight

Competitive Thinness: A competition-like mindset/situation regarding who is thinner. Typically occurs among females and involves body comparisons with others. This can result in body dissatisfaction and “motivation” to lose weight through numerous methods.

Contagion Effect: Social pressures to engage in behaviors (i.e., dieting, or undertaking extreme exercise regimens) which uphold norms of a team or other group.

Eating Disorders Not Otherwise Specified (EDNOS): The most prevalent eating disorder diagnosis. This is a residual category, comprising of the disorders that do not meet the full criteria for anorexia nervosa or bulimia nervosa.

Female Athlete Triad: A spectrum involving the three elements of energy (intake and expenditure), menstrual cycles, and bone health. When a female athlete does not properly fuel their body, they may experience chronic fatigue, anemia, lower bone density, and amenorrhea – the absence of three or more consecutive menstrual cycles.

What to Do

Destigmatize seeking mental health help by normalizing psychiatry and other therapy practices.

Emphasize fitness in terms of health and performance instead of weight and appearance.

Avoid the “more is better” approach when it comes to exercise and training regimens.

Encourage and provide nutritional education.

Highlight the role nutrition plays in athletic performance and as it relates to specific athletic goals.

Refrain from group weigh-ins and setting body composition goals that are unrealistic.

Make sure your athletes know their health and well-being are your main concern, even above winning and performance.

Create a team environment of trust and support through promotion of open communication.

Integrate mental skills training that supports self-worth and healthy coping mechanisms.

Have a referral plan and resources in place.

Know the Signs & Symptoms

Irregular eating behaviors, such as restricting, binging, and purging.

Exercising much longer, more frequently, and intensely than is typical or required.

Strict conviction that losing weight/body fat will result in improved athletic performance.

Changes in mood, such as increased irritability.

Sudden decrease in performance and/or motivation.

Use or abuse of diet pills, laxatives, and edemas.

Sudden overuse of caffeine, stimulants, or pre-workout supplements to offset lack of energy.

Treatment & Therapy Options

Cognitive Behavior Therapy (CBT): A form of psychotherapy which involves changing maladaptive behaviors by adjusting irrational thoughts.

Enhanced Cognitive Therapy (CBT-E): A highly individualized version of CBT, involving four distinct stages of treatment. The stages include personalized education, plan development, setbacks and mindset, and maintenance.

Dialectical Behavior Therapy (DBT): A form of psychotherapy, which uses acceptance or validation based on coping strategies, such as mindfulness, stress tolerance, and regulation of emotions.

Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT): A form of psychotherapy that utilizes mindfulness, commitment, and behavior change strategies to foster phycological flexibility. ACT focuses on six core process to achieve this main goal, including living in the present moment, diffusion, acceptance, self-as-context, values, and action commitment.

Narrative Therapy: A form of psychotherapy that helps patients effectively confront future issues through identification of possessed skills, abilities, and values.

Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing (EMDR): An evidence-based trauma therapy that uses bilateral eye movement or hand-buzzers for treatment of symptoms associated with trauma, such as PTSD, anxiety, and depression.

Yoga: A physical, mental, and/or spiritual practice that aims to control and still the mind.

Meditation: A practice which aims to clear the mind and calm emotions through various practices, or techniques. Some techniques include mindfulness and breath work.
